In The Game of Life, players spin a wheel to move across colored tiles to simulate adult life, whizzing around the board represented by a little plastic peg in the front seat of a little plastic car. At the start of the game, you choose between college and a career. Later, you hit a junction on a red tile and you have to make an important decision about your job, marriage, or house. Your choices cascade into each other throughout the game, and you often have to budget your money or navigate the luck of the roll. But at the end of the game, each player will have collected the various milestones of an adult life under their belt, from a diploma, to homeownership, to maybe a family, from all the red tiles they hit along the way.

  

In 2007, the New York Times Opinion columnist David Brooks [wrote](https://www.nytimes.com/2007/10/09/opinion/09brooks.html) about a new red tile milestone in people’s lives. He talks about how more and more people are experiencing a longer and longer period of “wandering” between adolescence and adulthood. Brooks calls this phase “the odyssey years.”

  

Much like Odysseus, our dear Core Curriculum hero, students spend a lot of time trying to figure out our lives and find our way back to a state of being home on the heels of a great struggle. While attending Columbia and job-hunting isn’t exactly like fighting in the Trojan War, there are some notable similarities between them. Consider the amount of Greek architecture around us, the struggle of resisting McKinsey’s siren song, and the number of times you have to tell men at bars, “I have a boyfriend, he’s just not here right now…” If anything, isn’t the last-ditch effort of hiding an invasion force inside a large wooden horse the equivalent of hoping a really good final group project saves your grade?

  

It has been 17 years since Brooks published his piece about the odyssey years, and much has changed to blow the traditional journey from childhood to adolescence to adulthood to old age even further off course. Brooks was writing before the Great Recession. He could never have fully predicted the subsequent years of economic and social trouble, the pandemic, or the existence of a multibillion [Youtube personality who rents out warehouses](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCX6OQ3DkcsbYNE6H8uQQuVA) to do massive social experiments for views. But he was right when he said that the freedom people feel when they lead “improvising lives” is both a blessing and a curse. We are not little plastic pegs; we have no colorful roadmap, no tiny automobiles. Now, more than ever, we are what we make of the world. It becomes more important to think about what makes us who we are and how that guides where we are going.

  

Thinking of this wandering time as an epic journey gives me comfort. I like knowing that *feeling* unmoored right now is because we are actively *being* unmoored. It’s fine to have to just figure things out for a bit. In fact, I contend it’s actually *exciting* to go and figure out who you are, to assume different roles and masks while figuring out how each of them suits who you want to become. Maybe I should learn how to do pottery, or how to make a three-course meal from scratch. I could try my hand at playing pool or discovering a favorite sandwich spot in the city. Or maybe I put my time into finding new community spaces, investing in my friendships, and calling my grandparents. Whatever I do, I should know it’s okay to be making things up as I go. I should be leading an improvisational life.

  

At this point, you may be asking: “Ning, if I am meant to lead an improvisational life on my own terms, why would I be listening to you and your score?”

  

Reader, I’ll be honest: Perhaps my column is just a glorified soapbox. Maybe our tales in life will be so different that there is no point in hearing them out, they would never apply to you. Or, conversely, they could be so similar that all that I have to say is redundant to you. Maybe there is no merit in my reflections and musings, in my personal search for meanings linking the events of my life together like a bizarre string of misshaped sausages. However, I hope there’s something in there that calls out to you, where you can feel a kind of kinship in the messiness, where you can rejoice in sharing the lightbulb moments, where you can feel your brain “cooking,” as my friend Bella calls it.

  

Over the course of this semester, I hope to talk about the lessons I’ve learned. I plan to share the things that bring me joy and the things that made me think harder about myself and my place in the world. I want to share this process of collaging together aspects of my life that make me and guide me, both the puzzle pieces that fit and the ones that don’t. Most of all, I hope I can leave you feeling a bit more settled, more braced to face the rest of your epic journey.

  

*Ning Chang is a senior in the dual B.A. program between Sciences Po and the School of General Studies majoring in human rights, and a former senior associate editor for Spectator’s Opinion page. In a conversation with Ning, you can’t escape without learning that she’s a native New Yorker or hearing at least three of her strongest opinions. She might be somewhat hypocritical, then, for writing a column about reevaluating your opinions and reshaping your sense of self. But hey—why not listen for a bit to a woman of many ways, to a fellow sailor of these wine-dark seas?*
